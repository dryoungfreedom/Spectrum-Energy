Retrofit vs New Build
RETROFIT (existing reactors):
Replace biological shield sections during scheduled outages. No core modifications. No coolant system changes. The transformer layers are the same mass and thickness as the concrete they replace — structural load unchanged. Phased installation: start with one section, prove it, expand.

NEW BUILD (future reactors):
Design the containment from the start with active transformer layers. Optimize layer thicknesses for the specific reactor's photon spectrum. Integrate the monitoring data bus into the plant control system. Include Cobalt-59 activation targets in the neutron reflector for intentional Spectrum Energy Cell fuel production.

The retrofit path proves the concept. The new-build path realizes the full potential.

Materials used in active shielding are commercially available today: CdTe and CZT detectors are manufactured by Redlen Technologies, Kromek, and others for medical imaging and security screening. NaI(Tl) scintillator crystals are standard in nuclear medicine. PbTe and SiGe thermoelectrics are used in spacecraft (RTGs) and industrial waste heat recovery. Tungsten heavy alloys are standard reactor shielding. No new materials need to be invented.

Monitoring resolution: CdTe detectors achieve ~1% energy resolution at 662 keV (Cesium-137 line). Position-sensitive arrays can map gamma flux with centimeter-scale spatial resolution. This is higher resolution than any current reactor monitoring system.

Power independence: The active shielding layers generate electricity from the radiation they absorb. This means the monitoring system is self-powered by the very radiation it measures. No external power source can be lost because none is needed. This addresses the single most critical failure mode in reactor safety — loss of monitoring during loss of power events.
